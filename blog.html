The 2027 NBRC Exam Is Coming — Here's What Every RT Student Needs to Know

CL
Caleb Littlejohn, RRT, CRT
· March 10, 2026 · 8 min read · ✦ Featured
⚡ TL;DR: The NBRC is replacing the TMC + CSE two-exam system with a single unified Respiratory Therapy Examination (RTE) launching January 2027. If you're close to testing, sit the current exam now. If you're further out, start building clinical reasoning skills today — not just exam facts.

If you're an RT student or a working therapist, you've probably heard the rumors: the NBRC is overhauling its certification exams in 2027. This isn't a minor update. It's the most significant restructuring of respiratory therapy credentialing in decades — and if you're prepping right now, what you do in the next 12–18 months matters more than you might think.

Here's everything you need to know, and what it means for how you study.

What's Changing in 2027

The current pathway to RRT certification requires passing two separate exams: the Therapist Multiple Choice (TMC) exam and the Clinical Simulation Exam (CSE). Starting in January 2027, the NBRC is replacing this two-step process with a single unified exam — the Respiratory Therapy Examination (RTE).

Feature Today (TMC + CSE) 2027+ (RTE)
Exam structure Two separate exams One unified exam
Credentialing TMC determines CRT/RRT eligibility; CSE for RRT One exam awards CRT/RRT based on total score
Question format MCQ + clinical simulations separately Combined breadth + clinical judgment questions
New content areas Traditional clinical domains Cultural awareness, ethics, trauma-informed care, new pharmacology
Emphasis Knowledge recall Clinical decision-making and reasoning

This is a fundamentally different kind of test. The TMC rewarded knowing facts. The 2027 RTE rewards knowing how to think like a respiratory therapist.

Why This Changes How You Need to Study

The old approach — memorizing tables, drilling flashcards, grinding practice tests — will not be enough for 2027.

The new exam is built around clinical decision-making. That means:

  • Context matters. Questions present patient scenarios, not isolated facts. You need to synthesize information, not just retrieve it.
  • Rationale over recall. You need to understand why an answer is correct — the pathophysiology, the clinical reasoning, the evidence base.
  • Ethics and communication are now core. Trauma-informed care, cultural competency, and end-of-life decision-making are tested domains, not electives.
  • Pharmacology is expanding. Newer evidence-based agents are replacing the traditional bronchodilator-heavy question bank. Expect unfamiliar drugs.
  • Scenario complexity is higher. You may see multi-step patient deterioration scenarios requiring sequential decision-making under pressure.

Students who start building clinical reasoning skills now — not just exam knowledge — will be ahead of everyone cramming in late 2026.

Should You Take the Current TMC Before 2027?

Short answer: yes, if you're close.

If you're within 12 months of being eligible to test, it is generally in your interest to sit for the current TMC/CSE pathway before the 2027 transition. The current exam is a known quantity. The 2027 RTE, while announced, still has evolving specifics — and first-year pass rates for new exam formats historically dip as students and programs adjust.

That said — the clinical skills you build preparing for either exam transfer completely. Strong clinical reasoning helps you pass both. Start there.
